University-Assisted Community Schools

A university-assisted community school is a model of partnership between universities and public schools. These innovative partnerships, grounded in the philosophy of education pioneer John Dewey, aim to transform local schools into central neighborhood establishments that inspire cooperation, deliver a host of services, and tackle pressing challenges faced by schools and communities in today's ever-changing global landscape (Harkavy, Hartley, Hodges & Weeks, 2013).

The UACS model has a multifaceted mission: to enhance student achievement, minimize absenteeism, boost graduation rates, and promote active community participation. It is built on the belief that schools can act as community centers and education is a shared responsibility.

However, for public schools to effectively fulfill this role, careful coordination across local, state, and federal government levels, along with engagement from nongovernmental agencies, is crucial. We must also strategically leverage the vast resources and expertise that higher educational institutions possess to support student learning, strengthen families, and rejuvenate communities. Doing so can provide the substantial support necessary for community schools to thrive, and to embrace the expanded roles we envision for them in the fabric of American society.

Over the past half-century, university-assisted community schools have made remarkable strides across the United States, a success documented by scholars in all regions (Jim Grim, Monica A. Medina & Nicole Y. Oglesby, 2020; xxxxxxx). As the newest regional technical training center for university-assisted community schools, SRCUACS aims to catalyze the further expansion of this innovative model throughout the Southeast region. We are dedicated to empowering universities and their partner schools with the necessary tools and support to establish community schools within their respective localities, thus continuing the transformative impact of this educational model.

For the history of the UACS movement, see the timeline below.

1960s-70s

The federal government introduced various initiatives to strengthen the connection between universities and their communities, including the establishment of Urban-Grant Universities and the development of Community Action Programs. During this time, the idea of the "communiversity" also emerged, emphasizing the importance of reciprocal and mutually beneficial partnerships between universities and local communities.

1994-2004

23 universities, including the Netter Center at the University of Pennsylvania, and 75 other institutions received funding from foundations and the federal government to promote the expansion of the university-assisted community schools model.

2008

University of Oklahoma-Tulsa selected as the site of first regional training center for university-assisted community schools, establishing the Higher Education Forum of Oklahoma.

2011

Indiana University—Purdue University Indianapolis (IUPUI) selected as the site of the second regional training center for university-assisted community schools, establishing the Midwest Center for University-Assisted Community Schools. There are now over 215 community schools in Indiana, with 77 in Indianapolis. IUPUI publishes their academic journal on community-engaged research, ENGAGE!, three times per year.

2014

University of Connecticut selected as the site of third regional training center, establishing the New England University-Assisted Community School Collaborative. Now known as CommPACT Community Schools Collaborative, there are currently 4 CommPACT-affiliated community schools across Connecticut.

2015

University-Assisted Community Schools Network formed as a partnership between the Netter Center (University of Pennsylvania), Rutgers University-Camden, and the Coalition for Community Schools. There are now over 70 universities in the network.

2017

UCLA selected as the site of fourth regional training center, establishing the UCLA Center for Community Schooling. They operate two community schools in Los Angeles— Mann UCLA Community School and RFK UCLA Community School. At least once a year, they publish an issue of the Community Schooling, a journal highlighting work in the community schools space across the country.

2020

Binghamton University (SUNY) selected as the site of fifth regional training center, supporting programs across New York and New Jersey. Binghamton recently received two five-year grants from the U.S. Department of Education, totaling $9,300,000, to expand mental health services in New York’s Southern Tier as part of its Mental Health Service Provider Demonstration Grant Program. For those interested in furthering their knowledge on community schools, Binghamton offers an online Community Schools Certificate.

2023

Duke, NCCU, and ECU selected as the site of sixth regional training center, forming the Southern Regional Coalition for University-Assisted Community Schools.
