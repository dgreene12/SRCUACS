Coalition Building

SRCUACS is a three-institution collaboration between East Carolina University, Duke University, and North Carolina Central University. Coalition building is the starting point and is foundational to this proposal. Each institution possesses unique and strategic assets necessary for broadening and deepening relationships with the diverse higher education ecosystem across North Carolina and the Southeast region. By leveraging assets and respective relationships, SRCUACS envisions an opportunity to learn across differences while also creating context-specific professional learning communities.

The goal is to build a three-institution coalition drawing from the strength of our respective institutions, namely HBCUs, rural, and urban institutions. Training and technical assistance will be provided for regional institutions interested in connecting the core mission of their colleges and universities to the six pillars: Coalition Building, Storytelling, Connecting, Pipeline Development, Policy Change, and Research. The SRCUACS will organize statewide and regional UACS leaders through monthly virtual network calls, quarterly site visits, annual symposium gatherings, and an up-to-date web presence.

Storytelling

Our SRCUACS collaborative draws inspiration from UACS Regional Training Centers’ peer-reviewed publications and related public-facing initiatives including the Netter Center at UPenn’s Universities and Community Schools Journal, the UCLA Center for Community Schooling Journal, and IUPUI’s Community School’s Forward initiative. As a regional training center, SRCUACS expects to advance the work of community schools and their university partners by providing dynamic vehicles for practitioner storytelling about community schools. From drawing on experiences that range from getting clarity and buy-in on community school pillars, to organizing communities and schools to identify assets and needs, to the hard work of transforming school practices, each community school has its story to tell. Centering practitioner voices and perspectives lifts up community, educator, and caregiver stories and allows others considering adopting a community school model the opportunity to learn from those immersed in values and practices of community schools.

Our team is poised to facilitate story-sharing in diverse methods across several publication formats and via diverse platforms. Leveraging a range of presentation methods creates opportunities for SRCUACS collaborators and local practitioners to participate in action research, for example, to collect oral histories of community assets that may then be disseminated through podcasts. We will use the network to gather and share basic logistical information on building a community school; feature CS practitioner action research projects; contribute to the academic scholarship on community schools; influence policy change that advances community schools in the region; and survey and create resources to effect preparation pipelines for community school educators and coordinators across our region.

As a collective partnership, SRCUACS shares an intentional focus on community schools as a means of advancing community-aware, context-specific, justice-oriented, and anti-racist education. The collaboration of a public university attending to rural community school models; an HBCU focusing on community schools as a tool for serving all students better; and a private university in close collaboration with its urban public school district means we begin this center with a rich variety of stories to tell and we anticipate being a national leader in sharing these conversations and stories across all platforms.

Connecting

The sustainability of localized community school initiatives relies on creating and supporting collective impact committees (CIC). In order to fully address the needs of a community, CICs should be populated by a diverse group of representatives from the business and nonprofit sectors, as well as local government and university leaders. In this way, community schools are intentional about building connections. SRCUACS believes a regional community schools training center is uniquely positioned to provide technical support for CICs in many domains, including mobilizing community resources across different sectors and developing braided funding strategies.

This current proposal brings to the forefront a commitment shared among partner institutions to explore ways that universities might connect existing institutional strengths, initiatives, and resources with local public schools in mutually beneficial ways. In its foundational stages, Duke and North Carolina Central’s partnership envisioned a merging of institutional assets (e.g., big ideas such as research and service; knowledge branches such as sciences, law/criminal justice, and teacher preparation; and auxiliary supports including speech and language clinics, writing studios, etc.) to support Durham community schools’ self-identified goals and values. The Durham partnership believes a conscious effort to leverage university resources in direct response to the schools’ findings (e.g., via the Community Schools Listening Project) would position Duke and NCCU to be better partners not only for community schools being piloted in the district, but for the school district as a whole. Similarly, ECU’s involvement in this work grew out of its commitment to schools and communities in the Eastern NC region and it’s recognition of the role of universities as “connectors” (Morse, 2004; Johnson, Thompson, & Naugle, 2009) with the capacity and responsibility to link salient assets to identified needs.

SRCUACS intends to engage in asset mapping of the material, human, and organizational resources that the partner institutions have available to offer local community schools. Close examination of these assets will help the collaborative understand where duplication exists across the state and region and identify spaces for innovation. Data collected from this mapping project will result in a digital database of relevant resources found at partnering institutions, which can then be used to match local community schools' identified needs and serve as a starting point for collaborative programming. SRCUACS would create a home for this database as well as a mechanism by which university faculty and staff can continuously define and refine formalized collaborations with public school partners.

Pipeline Development

SRCUACS will develop and operationalize a transformative educational preparation pipeline to effectively recruit, prepare, and sustain Community School Practitioners (CSPs). SRCUACS will utilize existing partnerships with ~50 local education agencies (LEAs), including formal networks such as the Latham Clinical Schools Network, to develop a teacher residency model for state and regional educational preparation programs. Within the residency model for CSPs, teacher residents will have robust professional learning experiences in existing community schools and will be prepared onsite in a(n) university-assisted community school(s) with clinically aligned coursework.

North Carolina has significant shortages in educational professionals across licensure areas. Our partner LEAs need (a) highly qualified teachers who meet the requirements for full state licensure, (b) structures and supports that sustain a new teacher pipeline, and (c) structures and support for teacher retention. The need for expanding the pipeline and producing more education professions creates an opportunity for equipping those professionals with the knowledge, skills, and dispositions to contribute to the UACS model. Teacher residency programs are an effective, comprehensive model for educator preparation. Residency programs have been shown to expand recruitment of candidates of color to the profession and improve district goals around teacher diversity, impact, and innovation. Residencies also provide opportunities for experienced teachers to act as mentors, while developing highly effective new teachers who are able to impact student achievement and community engagement from the moment they become the teacher of record.

SRCUACS will develop organizing frameworks for curriculum and pedagogical strategies, including research- and practice-based standards that guide regional and state educational preparation programs to prepare racially diverse, historically literate teachers for service in locally situated university-assisted community schools. The development of a transformational, equity-focused CSP educator pipeline will positively influence leadership practices and community engagement. Ultimately, our goal is to ensure communities feel seen, heard, and supported by local public schools in North Carolina. As we expand with the addition of partners throughout the Southeast region, we will support those partners in achieving the same goal in their respective states.

Policy Change

SRCUACS will leverage its work to support policy changes that result in an increased number of community schools, more effective implementation of the UACS model, and enhanced professional preparation programs that produce individuals with the capacity to contribute to the UACS model. Policy change and ongoing support from policymakers will be essential to realizing the potential for the UACS model in North Carolina and the region. Engaging policymakers to educate them about the model and demonstrate its impact in achieving common objectives will be key, and elements of the proposed Center directly address that work.

SRCUACS will be well positioned to engage state policymakers owing to several key elements of the proposed work—specifically, diversity in the initial partner sites (e.g., sites in both urban and rural settings); the statewide network that facilitates interaction and shared learning among sites: the research agenda for the empirical work (focuses on key elements of the UACS model but is appreciative of the contextual characteristics of individual communities) and related communication plan for disseminating results (focuses on varied products and delivery models for varied audiences); and the larger networks with which SRCUACS is connected (increasing exposure and foster legitimacy with policymakers). Identifying allies in state government and communicating with policymakers about what UACS sites are doing, the results they are achieving, and how policy and policymakers can contribute will be a central and ongoing part of our work.

The statewide SRCUACS network with a central hub will facilitate communication and shared learning among community school and UACS sites. This is crucial to developing understandings of how a shared model can be effectively implemented in different settings.

Research

In 2018, Durham Public Schools (DPS) and the Durham Public Schools Foundation partnered to pilot four community schools as a part of the Bull City Community Schools Partnership. During this time, faculty and staff at Duke and North Carolina Central University developed the Durham University-Assisted Community Schools Research Collective (DUACSRC), an interdisciplinary research team tasked with exploring how these two universities could leverage resources necessary to address the needs of local public schools through the UACS strategy. The DUACSRC is supported by Duke’s Bass Connections and Data + programs.

The DUACSRC operates through a year-long vertical integration model, engaging undergraduate students, graduate students, faculty, staff, and community partners in action-research methods. The overall research experience is rooted in interpretive social science methodologies that help to identify and investigate needs identified by regional community schools. Their work is also informed through annual site visits to UACS sites across the US which provides undergraduate researchers and faculty/staff leaders insight into the ongoing work of CS and UACS. Each year, the DUACSRC consolidates their findings and presents resources at the Bass Connections and Data+ showcase. A model for action-research such as the one described promotes student-led advocacy and community participation through facilitating an empowering research infrastructure.

Community-based action research is a powerful tool that can support the work of these coalitions by generating data-driven insights into the effectiveness of the UACS strategy and identifying areas for improvement. Additionally, community-based action research plays an essential role in building relationships between communities, universities, and other partners. The vertical integration model offers an important infrastructure for engaging both undergraduate and graduate students, allowing them to utilize expertise from their programs of study. It will also allow for the development of practitioners who are skilled and empowered to work with their communities; expanding capacity for engaged action researchers in the field focusing on informing ongoing UACS work.

While only recently launched, the ECU partnership with Elizabeth City-Pasquotank Schools is utilizing community-based action research with approaches that closely parallel the DUACSRC work to generate insights about the UACS strategy in Elizabeth City and–more specifically–about the nuances of implementing UACS in a rural setting.

The SRCUACS will serve to better guide and connect interdisciplinary UACS teams interested in launching community-based action research projects grounded in CS inquiry and strategy.
